## Research Background

The reason this theory was established is that when performing mechanical analysis on materials containing cracks and voids under loading, it is almost impossible to obtain analytical mathematical solutions. This is mainly because many materials are anisotropic polycrystals, and the various complex defects within the material are also very difficult to describe. One can only use numerical methods to solve for the true mechanical state of the material. The phase field method happens to be able to solve some single/polycrystal problems of materials. If it could also solve for the internal mechanical state of the material, that would be great. This leads to the main content of this paper.

## The Mysterious KS Theory

The establishment of the model primarily relies on the Khachaturyan-Shatalov (KS) theory (i.e., the theory pioneered by the corresponding author (the big shot) and another (obviously) Russian big shot). This theory transforms the problem of cracks/voids under applied stress $\sigma_{ij}^{\mathrm{appl}}$ into an equivalent different problem: a continuous, elastically homogeneous matrix doped with macroscopically homogeneous but mesoscopically heterogeneous stress-free strain caused by lattice mismatch $\epsilon_{ij}^o(\mathbf{r})$ (the renowned **Eigenstrain**).

### Frightening Formulas

This theory provides the exact solution for the strain energy functional and stress distribution, where the stress is:

$$
\begin{align*}
\sigma_{ij}(\mathbf{r}) &= C_{ijkl}\Bigg[\tilde\int\frac{d^{3}k}{(2\pi)^{3}}\,n_{k}\,\Omega_{lm}(\mathbf{n})\,\tilde\sigma^{o}_{mn}(\mathbf{k})\,n_{n}\,e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}}
-\bar\epsilon^{o}_{kl}(\mathbf{r})\Bigg]+ \sigma^{\mathrm{appl}}_{ij}
\end{align*}
$$

This formula is quite frightening, with many symbols... The integral sign with a small wavy line above it indicates integration in reciprocal space (i.e., performing a Fourier transform on the original space). The integration region is the infinite region with a volume of $(2\pi)^3/V$ around $\mathbf{k} = 0$ removed. $\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{k}/k$ should be the normal vector in reciprocal space. $\Omega_{ij}(\mathbf{n})$ is the so-called Green's function tensor, the inverse tensor of $\Omega^{-1}_{ij}(\mathbf{n}) = C_{ijkl}n_kn_l$, where $C_{ijkl}$ is the elastic modulus. $\tilde{\sigma}_{ij}^{o}(\mathbf{k}) = C_{ijkl}\,\tilde{\epsilon}_{kl}^{o}(\mathbf{k})$. The superscript $*$ in the formula denotes taking the complex conjugate. Finally, we have:

$$
\begin{align*}
\tilde{\epsilon}_{ij}^{0}(\mathbf{k}) &= \int_{V} \epsilon_{ij}^{0}(\mathbf{r})\, e^{-i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}}\, d^{3}r\\
\epsilon^{o}_{ij} &= \frac{1}{V}\int_{V}\epsilon^{o}_{ij}(\mathbf{r})\,\mathrm{d}^3r
\end{align*}
$$

Let's examine this formula. First, stress distribution is a scalar field, naturally obtained by "multiplying" the elastic modulus by the tensor generated by the strain inherently present in the material (actually a contraction), plus an externally applied stress. The $C_{ijkl}$ here is not problematic. The key lies in how to compute the strain tensor that follows. We can see that this strain consists of three parts: a complex reciprocal-space integral result, minus the above-mentioned macroscopically homogeneous but microscopically heterogeneous eigenstrain $\epsilon_{kl}^{o}(\mathbf{r})$, plus the average of this strain.

Looking at the integral again, inside there is a so-called Green's function tensor that depends only on the direction, followed by $\tilde\sigma^{o}_{mn}(\mathbf{k})$, which is itself obtained by multiplying the elastic modulus by some strain. And what is this strain? It is the Fourier transform of that eigenstrain. Finally, it is multiplied by the inverse transform factor to return to real space. That is to say, of the three parts composing this strain, the first part kneads and squeezes the eigenstrain in reciprocal space, presumably obtaining some kind of "averaging" property, then subtracting the un-kneaded part yields the heterogeneous main term, and finally adding the average strain yields the internal strain distribution field of the material.

To be honest, this qualitative analysis here is also just my wild guess. I'll need to look at the original literature later to understand what this so-called KS theory is really about, and why the strange integral in reciprocal space is used to knead and squeeze that strain.

### Energy Minimization Condition

Anyway, after obtaining the stress distribution, we can express the elastic energy. Taking the variational derivative of the elastic energy with respect to strain yields:

$$
\begin{align*}
\frac{\delta E^{\mathrm{el}}}{\delta \epsilon_{ij}^{o}(\mathbf r)} &= -\sigma_{ij}(\mathbf r)
\end{align*}
$$

Let's further consider this eigenstrain here. It should only be non-zero within regions of the continuum where there is mismatch, and should be $0$ everywhere else. And if the eigenstrain field is the special distribution $\epsilon^{oo}_{ij}(\mathbf{r})$ that minimizes the elastic energy, then:

$$
\begin{align*}
\left.\frac{\delta E^{\mathrm{el}}}{\delta \epsilon^{o}_{ij}(\mathbf{r})}\right|_{\epsilon^{o}_{ij}(\mathbf{r})=\epsilon^{oo}_{ij}(\mathbf{r})}=0
\end{align*}
$$

And because the eigenstrain can only be non-zero inside the mismatch (distortion) regions, naturally that special energy also satisfies this condition. Comparing the two formulas above, we can find that inside the mismatch regions, the value of the stress field should all be $0$. Therefore, taking this back to that very troublesome equation, we have:

$$
C_{ijkl}\Bigg[ \tilde\int\frac{d^3k}{(2\pi)^3}\, n_k\,\Omega_{lm}( \mathbf{n})\,\tilde{\sigma}^{oo}_{mn}(\mathbf{k})\,n_n\,e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}_d}- \epsilon^{oo}_{kl}(\mathbf{r}_d) + \bar{\epsilon}^{oo}_{kl}\Bigg] + \sigma^{\mathrm{appl}}_{ij} = 0
$$

This is the condition obtained through energy minimization.

### Removing the Mismatch Region and the Equivalent Problem

Since there is no stress inside the mismatch regions at equilibrium, we can remove them without changing the internal stress state. And removing them is equivalent to creating voids and cracks! In this way, we have achieved this miraculous problem transformation process. This problem had already been solved in Khachaturyan's previous work on the problem of distributed mismatch-generated strain in elastically homogeneous matrices[^2]. The preceding exposition demonstrates that *the elastic strain and strain energy of an elastically homogeneous matrix system with mismatch-generated strain are actually identical to the elastic strain and strain energy in a discontinuous system containing voids and cracks, provided that the mismatch strain of the latter minimizes the elastic energy of the system*. This provides the theoretical basis for us to use the phase field method to study *energy minimization problems*.

## Phase Field Model

Based on the above situation, we naturally use the Allen-Cahn model:

$$
\begin{align*}
\frac{\partial \epsilon^{o}_{ij}(\mathbf{r},t)}{\partial t} &= -L_{ijkl}\frac{\delta E^{\mathrm{el}}}{\delta \epsilon^{o}_{kl}(\mathbf{r},t)}
\end{align*}
$$

where $L_{ijkl}$ is the kinetic factor, and the rest have already been explained. The results obtained through the phase field method agree with those systems for which analytical solutions exist.

### Another Approach

Additionally, there is another phase field equation that can be used to solve this problem. However, we need to change the description of the eigenstrain. Under this description, it possesses invariant plane strain[^5] (? what the heck):

$$
\begin{align*}
\epsilon_{ij}^{o}(\mathbf{r}) &= \sum_{\alpha=1}^{p} h_i(\alpha,\mathbf{r})\,H_j(\alpha)
\end{align*}
$$

where $\mathbf{H}(\alpha)$ is a reciprocal space vector representing several possible cleavage planes; $\mathrm{h}(\alpha,\mathrm{r})$ is the crack opening vector, used to describe the displacement discontinuity under $\alpha$-type cleavage. These two quantities can be viewed as long-range non-conserved order parameters, capable of describing general configurations with arbitrarily mixed cracks. However, its strain energy and eigenstrain are still given by the KS theory.

To describe the force resisting crack opening, one must describe its strain energy. This energy can be described using a "coarse-grained" Landau energy:

$$
\begin{align*}
E^{\mathrm{ch}} &= \sum_{\alpha=1}^{p} \int_{V} f^{\mathrm{ch}}\bigl[\,h(\alpha,\mathbf{r})\,\bigr]\,\mathrm{d}^{3}\mathbf{r}.
\end{align*}
$$

Here, $f^{\mathrm{ch}}[\mathbf{h}(\alpha)]$ describes the effect of continuous breaking of atomic bonds on the cleavage plane during the opening process. Additionally, the gradient energy used to represent the curvature of the crack surface is expressed as:

$$
\begin{align*}
E^{\mathrm{grad}} &= \sum_{\alpha=1}^{p}\int_{V}\frac{1}{2}\,D_{ijkl}(\alpha)\,[\mathbf{H}(\alpha)\times\nabla]_i\,h_j(\alpha,\mathbf{r})\,[\mathbf{H}(\alpha)\times\nabla]_k\,h_l(\alpha,\mathbf{r})\,d^{3}r
\end{align*}
$$

Another complex integral, and there's even a cross product!? This energy will be $0$ at smooth crack surfaces and will contribute some energy at crack tips. Finally, we can construct the total energy by simply summing up the elastic energy, the chemical bond breaking energy, and this gradient energy:

$$
\begin{align*}
E &= E^{\mathrm{el}} + E^{\mathrm{ch}} + E^{\mathrm{grad}}.
\end{align*}
$$

If we replace the energy and eigenstrain in the Allen-Cahn form of the equation with the energy and crack opening vector from the new approach, we obtain the phase field model previously used by the authors to describe martensitic phase transformations[^3][^4][^5].

## Summary

Frightening equations and frightening energy constructions, yet this pathway can miraculously unify the mechanical situation of continuous bodies with that of discontinuous bodies, making one energy applicable everywhere, and this energy minimization approach happens to perfectly combine with the phase field method — this point is simply too brilliant. If only the free energy form of this method were a bit simpler, that would be even better.
