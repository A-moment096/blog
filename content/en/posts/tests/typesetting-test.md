It was just past six in the morning when the sparrows outside the window began their incessant chirping—which proved more effective than any alarm clock; I rubbed my eyes, sat up, and glanced at the chaotic mess on my desk: scattered sheets of paper, a handful of used pens, half a cup of cold tea from last night, and a worn-out copy of “The Oxford Dictionary” that had clearly seen better days (its spine was cracked, and several pages were dog-eared). All these objects bore silent witness to the frantic all-night writing session—the kind that leaves you both exhausted and oddly exhilarated. Then, as I stumbled toward the bathroom, the water heater suddenly broke down with a shrill beep—“Didi Didi”—and I had no choice but to splash cold water on my face while muttering, “Is today going to be one of those days?!” But then I remembered: the deadline was tonight (the editor’s email was crystal clear on that point), so I had no room for superstition or delay. I brewed a fresh cup of strong tea, settled down, and opened my document; the sections that needed extra examples (such as dialect survey data, or precise citations from ancient texts) and the transitions that required polishing—I marked all of them in red; as for the final sentimental remarks, I simply deleted them, because my editor always says, “Good writing is made through revision, not through sighing”—and she’s absolutely right. By the time my instant noodles turned cold, the sky had brightened considerably... and the blinking cursor on the screen seemed to ask, “Are you ready yet...?” That string of questions, coupled with the birds outside, the tea stains in my cup, and the faint ink smudges on the paper, lent this ordinary morning an oddly solemn atmosphere—almost as if the universe itself were waiting for my next keystroke; and yet, I couldn’t help but wonder: would this draft finally meet the editor’s exacting standards, or would I have to start all over again tomorrow?

There is one thing I've learned from my daily commute: the high-speed rail (高铁) is remarkably punctual, yet the real adventure begins when you step off the train—especially in a city like Chengdu, where the aroma of hotpot (火锅) and the chatter of locals (四川话) fill the air. 不过，每次我试图用普通话点餐时，总会闹出些笑话：比如把“担担面”说成“蛋蛋面”，或者把“兔头”误称为“吐头”——这些口误引得老板哈哈大笑，然后他便会用半生不熟的英语回我：“No problem, sir! You want spicy or not spicy?” 这时，我常常思考：语言学习是否真的需要完美？或者，那些错误反而让交流更有温度？I've also noticed that younger Chinese speakers love to sprinkle English abbreviations into their conversations, such as “AI”, “PK”, and “VIP”—which sound quite trendy, but sometimes confuse older generations who prefer full translations like “人工智能” or “对决”. 更有趣的是，当我尝试用英文写日记时，总忍不住插入几句中文成语，比如“画蛇添足”、“对牛弹琴”或“掩耳盗铃”——这些成语的意境实在难以用英文准确传达，不是吗？……而当我回头重读那些混杂的句子时，竟觉得它们别有一番风味，仿佛两种语言在纸上跳起了探戈；最终，我决定不再纠结对错，只管写下心中所想——无论是英文的流畅、中文的凝练，还是两者交织出的那种“破格”之美，都值得珍藏。
