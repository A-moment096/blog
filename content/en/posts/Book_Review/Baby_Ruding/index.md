*Written on September 22. Since this is the first proper math book I've ever finished reading, I felt a strong need to jot something down — hence this rambling note. Being the very first post in the Mathematics category feels just right.*
<!--more-->
*Principles of Mathematical Analysis*, written by Walter Rudin (May 2, 1921 – May 20, 2010), is a textbook on mathematical analysis "[suitable for advanced undergraduates or first-year graduate students in mathematics](https://www.zhihu.com/question/559683007/answer/3502165974)." Among Rudin's three analysis textbooks (the other two being *Real and Complex Analysis* and *Functional Analysis*), this one is the "smallest," hence the nickname **Baby Rudin** (the other two are called *Papa/Big Rudin* and *Grandpa Rudin*, respectively). As the first proper math book I've read cover to cover (while doing almost none of the exercises), it took me over a year of on-and-off reading to finally finish. A bit embarrassing, honestly — a book meant for undergrads, yet I didn't get through it until my second year of graduate school. Good thing I'm not a math major. As a complete outsider, I'd like to share some thoughts with a joyful heart.

Even though I've been casually reading math books since junior year of college, the ones I'd say I've "seriously read" probably amount to just *Calculus* (by Fikhtengol'ts), *Algebra: Chapter 0* (by Paolo Aluffi), *An Introduction to Manifolds* (by Loring W. Tu), and this *Baby Rudin*. The rest I've mostly just flipped through without going deep. I once thought about carefully reading the famous *GTM 73* by Thomas W. Hungerford, but I couldn't stick with it. In the end, only *Baby Rudin* was read front to back almost everywhere (by "almost" I mean all but countably many exercises). Still, all those browsed books have given me some courage — or perhaps the audacity — to pass judgment on a proper math textbook.

The book embodies "economy of ink" on every page. Unlike many other authors who favor formalistic language (e.g., using triple notation like $\left( X, A, \mu \right)$ to denote a measure space), Rudin is remarkably restrained with symbols. What surprised me most: it isn't until the third section of the very last chapter that Rudin finally introduces set-builder notation $\\{ x \vert P \\}$. Analysis may not involve the vast zoo of sets with wildly different elements that algebra does, but to hold off on such common notation until that late — that truly deserves the label "economical with words." But from another angle, perhaps it is precisely this restraint with symbols, this preference for explaining mathematical concepts in plain prose, that gives *Baby Rudin* its unique charm.

The organization of the book is also, in my view, unconventional. While constructing the real numbers, it casually handles complex numbers along the way, even dedicating a separate section at the end of Chapter 1 to a thorough construction of the reals. Then Chapter 2 doesn't rush into sequences or limits; instead, it first introduces the concept of functions, builds up point-set topology on top of sets and functions, and the word "limit" first appears as "limit points" in the topological sense rather than the traditional sequential limit. Also worth mentioning: the word "topology" only appears in the chapter title and in two sentences about triangulation elsewhere in the book — in the main text of Chapter 2, which is all about topology, the word itself never shows up once. A chapter on topology without the word "topology," yet through the introduction of distance (metric) it concretely discusses the kind of topological spaces that actually matter for analysis — a truly novel reading experience. The early introduction of topological concepts profoundly shapes everything that follows. One advantage is a finer depiction of the relationship between topology and analysis. For instance, the chapter on continuity actively employs the equivalent characterization "the preimage of an open set is open."

Having laid the groundwork of number fields and topology, what greets the reader next is not just sequences — the usual vehicle for introducing limits of functions — but also series, introduced together in the same breath. Unlike many textbooks that push series to later chapters, this approach not only introduces convergence earlier but also better connects "limits of sequences" with "infinite series," immediately applying the topological concepts from the previous chapter to reveal the relationship between completeness and sequences.

Among the three pillars of calculus (differentiation, integration, sequences/series of functions), the most distinctive part has to be integration: after a brief introduction to the Riemann integral, the book broadly discusses the Riemann–Stieltjes integral, and thanks to the earlier introduction of topology and completeness, it explores complete function spaces. Typical textbooks tend to dwell on the properties of the Riemann integral before jumping straight to the Lebesgue integral much later. Here, however, after defining the Riemann integral, Rudin immediately presents the more general Riemann–Stieltjes integral and discusses its properties in depth. Moreover, thanks to the introduction of metric and completeness, the section on sequences of functions also discusses the topological properties of function spaces, and the final Stone–Weierstrass theorem further approaches function approximation by polynomials, studying the space of polynomials and the space of functions from an algebraic perspective. Every one of these features was an eye-opener for me.

After covering the standard calculus material, Rudin finally decides to talk about some common, somewhat special functions (not the "special functions" of *special function theory*). The most interesting bit is surely the definition of trigonometric functions: instead of the usual approach, Rudin actively uses complex numbers and complex functions — introduced early in the book — defining trigonometric functions via the complex exponential. In an unexpected move, he gives a definition of $\pi$ and then tells the reader: we are now in a position to easily prove the algebraic completeness of the complex number field, which he does in about a page. Remarkable.

In the multivariable functions section, what impressed me most was that it resolved a long-standing question of mine: what exactly is the **derivative** of a multivariable function (not the partial derivative)? After introducing the concept of linear maps — seemingly unrelated to calculus — Rudin presents the result of differentiating a multivariable function: a linear map from $\mathbb{R}^n$ to $\mathbb{R}^n$! This insight took my understanding of mathematical generalization a step further. Aside from this enlightening bit, the rest felt rather opaque. The proofs of the Implicit Function Theorem and the Rank Theorem in particular were, to my eyes, practically hieroglyphics. In the end, with some help from the internet, I stumbled through them with a vague sense of understanding and fled from this section.

The last two chapters are the "One More Thing" part typical of analysis textbooks: integration of differential forms, and the Lebesgue integral. Integration of differential forms feels a bit pale without the machinery of manifolds to support it, but as a supplement to the integration of multivariable and vector-valued functions, Stokes' Theorem still delivers its characteristically beautiful conclusion. As for the Lebesgue integral, I finally got to understand the similarities and differences between the celebrated Lebesgue integral and the Riemann integral. The introduction of the $\mathcal{L}^2$ space at the end of the Lebesgue section also answered my question: why does harmonic analysis start from $\mathcal{L}^2$ space, and what makes it so special? Functions in $\mathcal{L}^2$ space, given a basis (a class of orthogonal functions), always correspond one-to-one with a square-convergent series — in other words, $\mathcal{L}^2$ is an infinite-dimensional linear space whose elements are functions, equipped with the $L^2$ norm.

All in all, *Baby Rudin* is a book that opened my eyes in every possible way. Without question, it gave me a new perspective from which to view analysis — whether it's the topological introduction, differentiation of multivariable functions, integration of differential forms, or the Lebesgue integral, the new concepts and the connections forged within these pages have enriched my vision. That said, even the main text has its challenging passages, and the restrained use of symbols sometimes feels charmingly old-school, while other times it leaves me utterly disoriented. And, as they say, the real treasure lies in the exercises at the end of each chapter — which I merely skimmed, glancing at a few problems that might introduce new concepts or definitions, without really digging in. Perhaps I'm what you'd call a *terminology tourist* — reading without doing exercises, unlikely to learn anything of substance. But as an enthusiast, I suppose that's not such a big problem. My next book might be *Intro to Manifolds*, which I was reading before returning home, or *Chap 0*, which I was reading while still abroad — but most likely, I'll set math aside for a while.
