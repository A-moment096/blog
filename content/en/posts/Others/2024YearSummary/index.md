It's hard for me to say that 2024 went well. Racing from the first year of my master's into the second, and now having decided to pursue a PhD — so many people have helped me along the way, yet I feel increasingly lost. I can't help but doubt: was doing a master's, or a PhD, the right choice, or the wrong one?

Since the start of 2024, I've had a nagging feeling that maybe I'm not really cut out for research. Yet perhaps out of vanity, or maybe this so-called "academic ideal," or simply because I promised my advisor I'd do a PhD, I still signed up for the doctoral interview when the new academic year began. To be honest, the interview wasn't hard, nor was it a competitive selection exam — under normal circumstances it should've been easy to pass with light preparation. Yet when I passed, I couldn't feel happy at all. Probably because I could sense the pressure coming from the future.

Many people say I'm well-suited for research: I tend to work alone, I like to dig deep and get to the bottom of things, I can sit still for long periods, I'm smart, and so on. These assessments come from all sorts of people: family, friends, lab mates, professors, etc. It's hard to say their evaluations are wildly off — aside from "smart" being a bit embarrassing to claim, the rest don't seem inaccurate as descriptors of me. But jumping from these traits to the conclusion that I'm suited for research... I feel there's always some bias. Or maybe, I simply discovered I don't like research after actually engaging with it, rather than not being suited for it.

Regardless, I've still set off down this path, and I'll be walking it for at least four more years. Reality always slices ideals into thin slivers, fries and stews them until they puff up into mere wishes — academic ideals are no exception. When I first entered grad school, I talked about publishing in prestigious journals, producing remarkable research... Now I just hope to graduate smoothly. Especially after seeing several senior lab mates' pre-graduation anguish, running around frantically just to graduate — I hope even more that when my turn comes, I can be calm, graduate smoothly, land a decent job, and call it a day.

Despite all this gloom, 2024 actually had plenty of highlights and accumulation too. What I'm perhaps most proud of is this blog I've built, and the Phase Field tutorial series I made on Bilibili. People say labor brings happiness, and I genuinely felt this while writing blog posts and making tutorials. Even though the Phase Field tutorials hit some resistance, finally managing to land them made me really happy. Plus, these notes actually helped me in return: to produce the best content possible, I had to constantly delve deeper and learn more about a topic. In that process, I learned a huge amount — and I don't even have to worry about forgetting details, because I've written everything down in the blog. As a personal notebook site, it's probably my most meaningful personal project to date, and in a sense, my personal interpretation of the open-source spirit.

Another piece of perhaps-good news is that I still haven't lost my old interests and hobbies. You can probably tell from the blog. I still maintain my love for mathematics, and I still enjoy programming and computers. The bad news that comes with it, though, is that I've discovered — compared to materials science — I seem to like computers and programming even more. Maybe doing a PhD is like gambling: I'm betting I can graduate successfully and find a decent job. If I do graduate, then my accumulated knowledge in math, programming, and computers might help me find a good job; but if I fail to graduate, then these hobbies are just stumbling blocks in my way. I'd better keep a positive mindset — at least these interests can still, to some extent, be applied in my research.

Finally, if I had to give my second year of the master's a concluding statement, I'd say: this was the academic year that made me reacquaint myself with who I am, and the last year I can call myself a master's student. The trials of a PhD student await me, and I hope I'm ready.

---

Before starting this year's academic autobiography, I tried to find the ones I wrote before. Bad news: I can't seem to find what I wrote last year. I could only find what I wrote right when I started grad school — sunny, positive, and full of energy. Back then I was genuinely brimming with confidence. I wonder what that past me would think, seeing my current state? Would he decide to just finish the master's and run?

Today, the graduating master's students are taking photos downstairs. Everyone's jumping around excitedly, all smiles. If I weren't doing a PhD, would I be standing downstairs next year with a smile on my face too? That's all turned to bubbles now. But if I can push through and work hard, maybe in four or five years, I'll be standing under the San Yi Building, laughing too. Hahaha.

Honestly, this blog is supposed to be a tech-oriented blog, but since it's my personal blog, I can write however I want. I've thought about just writing diary entries directly on the blog, but that's a bit cumbersome: you'd need an editor that conveniently handles `Markdown`, reliable internet, and the ability to do simple previews of what you write. But writing the academic-year summary here feels pretty nice.

Still, since it's a tech blog, this post won't stay public for long. It'll only be online for 7 days, after which it'll be hidden. Partly to prevent the content from being too scattered and not resembling a tech blog; partly to protect what little privacy I have? Hahaha.
