I've decided to make September 22nd my blog's birthday.

Y? Isn't the earliest date on any of this blog's posts November 1st, 2024? Why choose September 22nd? Well, it goes back to this blog's prototype. If you've read the post about [building this blog](/posts/Build_Blog), you'll know I initially planned to use Hexo as my build tool. Unfortunately, my Hexo journey didn't go smoothly, and I eventually chose Hugo. Back when I first tried Hexo — although I didn't write about it — that actually happened during the National Day holiday.

So why not the National Day holiday, but September 22nd? The reasoning is actually simple. The first article of this blog, though not published on September 22nd on this blog, was written on September 22nd — this [Baby Rudin Reading Notes](/posts/baby_rudin/). In the end, everything on this blog was born out of the sense of accomplishment, or vanity, or the impulse to share after I finished reading Baby Rudin. And that article was the beginning of it all, setting the tone for this little site: a blog that seems to lean toward technical or theoretical content.

Why *seems to*? It's hard for me to say how technically deep or theoretically profound the content on this little site really is. I just share what I like — maybe one day I came across an [interesting problem](/posts/functional_derivative/), maybe I ran into an [interesting syntax quirk](/posts/vector_memory_layout/) while coding, or maybe I felt I should document some [handy command-line tricks](/posts/last_command/), or maybe I just hopped on a trend and [proved the Snake Lemma](/posts/snake_lemma/) in the Year of the Snake. The average quality of my blog's posts probably hasn't reached a level where I can proudly call it a *tech blog* — it's just me entertaining myself.

Even so, I still feel there are some posts from this past year that I'm quite proud of. The one I most want to bring up is the [Snake Lemma proof](/posts/snake_lemma/) I just mentioned. Looking at it now, that post has many problems, the biggest of which is that it's too \*\*\*ing long, making it hard for people to want to read through. The blog's estimated reading time is 75 minutes — a whole hour! And with mathematical content like that, I believe if someone really tried to read through it bit by bit, it'd very likely take more than an hour. And even if my dear readers have tremendous tolerance and are willing to read through this extremely long article, they'd still likely find the second problem: the content is too fragmented and too cluttered. It's not suitable for reading in one sitting; I clearly should have split it into at least three parts. But I just kept writing for the thrill of it, all the way until it reached 75 minutes of reading time. Now suppose further that a reader has enough time and energy to read this article — they might find a third problem: the specific content organization still has issues. There's too much filler talk; some concepts are mentioned repeatedly without hitting the nail on the head; the notation isn't quite standard either — most places use $\operatorname{ker}$ instead of $\operatorname{Ker}$. Finally, there are some less critical but visually annoying issues, like certain formatting problems. If you've read that post before, you'll notice some collapsed sections are hard to discover can be expanded; some content has weird indentation; some formulas even have rendering errors. These are all hard flaws in that post, and I hope I'll have the chance to reboot this series. If I do reboot it, I'll definitely write it starting from basic algebra, making the proof of the Snake Lemma as accessible as possible. Though, by then, whether I'll need to re-learn how to prove this lemma... that's another question, hahaha. Still, there are things I can improve now: the blog has added support for some math environments, and I'll update those as soon as possible, giving things like lemmas, theorems, and definitions a more polished appearance.

Yet this post isn't my "hottest" article — it might even be the quietest one. Based on the comments my blog posts have received, the most popular one is this article about [Functional Derivatives and Variational Calculus](/posts/functional_derivative/). That post originated from a small question a senior lab mate asked me before winter break, but it detonated the bomb of me *not really understanding variational calculus at all*. To solve my senior's question, and incidentally solve my own confusion, I consulted some references and wrote that article. To be honest, it's really just piggybacking on others' wisdom — without Professor Lao Dazhong's *Foundations of Variational Calculus*, there's no way I could have written that article as quickly and well as I did. That it's been received so well and has helped people genuinely gives me a sense of accomplishment. I'm really grateful for everyone's support.

Another thing I want to mention are the articles about Arch Linux. Though I've only written [two](/tags/arch/) posts directly related to Arch, and both are about my Arch Linux installation process, the biggest gain from this past year of building the blog — apart from the blog itself — has been my experiences using Arch Linux and the Linux operating system knowledge I've picked up along the way. And on this blog, almost all the command-related content came about because I actually started using Arch Linux, which led me to the idea of writing how-to-style articles for my own reference. Friends, Arch Linux is the way! GNU/Linux is the way! Open source is also the way!

Finally, I want to mention one series on my blog, which was also established right from the start: the Phase Field Simulation Notes series. This series originated from the Phase Field training sessions I ran for our group — though calling it "training" is a stretch; they're more like notes for my own review. The content is basically transcribed from English lecture slides I started with, so there might still be some errors and omissions. If there's a Phase Field training plan this semester, I might continue updating this article series.

I currently have an article in progress about the conceptual analysis of functions. It's more like I've been examining how various textbooks introduce the concept of a function — the most fundamental concept in mathematical learning. I might do some personal synthesis at the end, or maybe not, but having written up to this point, I've really discovered it's a fascinating topic. I hope I can finish it in the next couple of days. If you've read this first-anniversary commemorative post, please do look forward to the next article.

So, as always, to every reader who's made it here: I wish you good health and a happy life!
